Essay · June 18, 2026

The morning you get back

There is a version of your morning that does not exist yet. In it, you wake up and you already know. You know what moved overnight. You know who responded. You know what the day demands of you, and what it does not.

M
Matteo Ferenczi

There is a version of your morning that does not exist yet. In it, you wake up and you already know.

You know what moved overnight. You know who responded. You know what the day demands of you, and what it does not. You do not piece this together. It is simply there — organized, quiet, ready.

Right now, most people spend the first hour of their working day reconstructing context. They open five apps and read backward through time. They find the thing that mattered. They remember what was said. By the time they have a clear picture, the morning is compromised.

Tovo gives that hour back. It watches your connected sources overnight and by the time you open it, the brief is written. Not a summary of everything — a distillation of what matters. That's the difference.

I have been using it for three months. I am a slower writer in the afternoon than I used to be. It turns out I was spending afternoon energy on morning admin that had no business being there.

Personal · May 15, 2026

The morning ritual I didn't know I was missing

I am not someone who writes about productivity tools. I find most of them dehumanizing in a way I struggle to name precisely. They tend to treat the person using them as a system to be optimized rather than a person trying to do something meaningful with their time.

S
Solange Abiodun

I am not someone who writes about productivity tools. I find most of them dehumanizing in a way I struggle to name precisely. They tend to treat the person using them as a system to be optimized rather than a person trying to do something meaningful with their time.

I started using Tovo because a colleague would not stop mentioning it. Within a week I understood why.

The brief I receive in the morning feels like something a thoughtful person wrote for me. It knows that the message from my mother is more important than the newsletter from a service I subscribed to three years ago and forgot about. It knows that the thing I said I would do on Tuesday and did not do on Tuesday matters more than the meeting I already attended.

There is something respectful in it. The product seems to understand that my time and attention are finite and treats them accordingly. I am aware this sounds like a lot to say about an application. But we spend hours every day in these things. Whether they respect you is not a small question.

Opinion · May 8, 2026

I don't think Tovo is for everyone. Here's who it's actually for.

The pitch for Tovo is that it gives you your morning back. I want to push back on this slightly, not because it's wrong, but because it implies everyone's morning is the same problem. It isn't.

R
Remy Castellan

The pitch for Tovo is that it gives you your morning back. I want to push back on this slightly, not because it's wrong, but because it implies everyone's morning is the same problem. It isn't.

If your mornings are already structured — if you have a clear routine and your information sources are few — Tovo will feel like an elegant solution to a problem you don't have. That is fine. Not every product is for every person.

Tovo is for people whose information is distributed. Multiple inboxes. Multiple calendars. Projects that live in different places and conversations that span different platforms. It is for the person who, at 8:47am, genuinely does not know what needs to happen today until they have assembled that picture from seven different sources.

If that person is you, Tovo will feel like a relief so immediate it's almost embarrassing. I know because that person is me, and that is exactly what it felt like.

Essay · April 30, 2026

Tovo raised my expectations for every other app I use

This is the thing no one tells you about using a product that actually works: it makes every product that doesn't work more conspicuous. I am now aware of the friction in things I accepted as normal. The morning brief I had for six weeks has made me intolerant of apps that do not respect my time.

M
Matteo Ferenczi

This is the thing no one tells you about using a product that actually works: it makes every product that doesn't work more conspicuous.

I have been using Tovo for several months. I have noticed, in the weeks since, that I am aware of the friction in things I previously accepted as normal. The morning brief I receive has made me impatient with apps that require me to do the work of assembly. I have cancelled two subscriptions that I realized I kept only out of inertia.

There is a phrase I keep returning to: "earned trust." Tovo has earned mine, slowly, over a series of correct mornings. A correct morning is not a flashy one. It is a morning where I know exactly what I need to know, nothing more, nothing faster than I can use it.

I have stopped describing Tovo to people as an AI tool. I describe it as a briefing. The person who gives you the briefing happens to be very good at their job.

Commentary · April 18, 2026

Six months later: the apps I still use and the ones I stopped

I wrote about Tovo in June. Since then, several people have asked me whether I am still using it. The honest answer: yes, and it is the only AI-adjacent product from the last two years that I am still using. The others were experiments. This became infrastructure.

P
Preethi Subramaniam

I wrote about Tovo in June. Since then, several people have asked me whether I am still using it. The honest answer: yes, and it is the only AI-adjacent product from the last two years that I am still using.

The others were experiments. This became infrastructure. The test I apply to any productivity product is whether I notice its absence. When Tovo was offline for maintenance last month, I noticed. My morning felt like assembling a puzzle without the picture on the box.

I have also watched several colleagues adopt it over the past months. The reactions are consistent: immediate skepticism followed by quiet conversion. Nobody announces that they have become a believer. They just stop complaining about mornings.

My standing critique remains the onboarding. The product assumes more patience than new users tend to have. When something is this genuinely useful, it should make a stronger case for itself earlier. That is the only reason I do not recommend it to everyone immediately.
